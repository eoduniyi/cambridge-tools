The Traveling Flyerperson

An interactive introduction to route optimization in Cambridge, MA

cambridge-dev · May 2026

The Problem

You have a set of flyers for a local software group. You live in Cambridge, Massachusetts. 6.4 square miles of dense sidewalks, two research universities, and a long tradition of community bulletin boards. You want to post these flyers where people will actually see them, and you want to walk an efficient route to do it.

The question where should I place these flyers? opens floodgates to combinatorial optimization, survival analysis, and sequential decision-making. This article walks through the first door: the routing problem.

Routing as a Traveling Salesman Problem

Given n locations to visit, starting and ending at the Cambridge Public Library, what's the shortest route? This is the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP), one of the most studied problems in combinatorial optimization.

A tour is a permutation π of the locations with total cost:

C(π) = n−1∑i=1 d(πi, πi+1) + d(πn, π1)

We want the permutation that minimizes this cost. For our ~10 locations, three algorithmic strategies illustrate the tradeoffs:

Brute Force

Try every possible ordering. There are (n−1)!/2 distinct tours. For 10 locations that's ~181,000 tours. Feasible, but the approach collapses for larger inputs. This gives the exact optimal solution.

Nearest Neighbor

A greedy heuristic: from your current location, always go to the closest unvisited stop. Fast (O(n²)) but often produces routes 20 to 30 percent longer than optimal because it doesn't plan ahead.

2-Opt

Start with the Nearest Neighbor solution, then iteratively improve: pick two edges, reverse the segment between them, keep the change if it shortens the route. This is a local search method. No global guarantee, but in practice it gets close.

The Constraints

Cambridge isn't a blank grid. Massachusetts law prohibits posting on public utility poles, lamp posts, shade trees, and public buildings. A $300 fine per violation. This eliminates the most visible, highest-traffic locations.

Even on private property, most locations require permission. Cafes curate their boards. Libraries have posting policies. MIT reserves campus boards for recognized student groups. Business Improvement Districts employ crews that remove unauthorized postings daily.

The net effect: the set of valid flyering locations is much smaller than the set of visible ones, and survival time varies enormously by location type. A flyer in a quiet laundromat stays up for months. One on a transit shelter lasts hours.

Why are We Interested in This?

The routing problem is the entry point, but the deeper question is: which locations are actually worth visiting? Route optimization assumes you already know where to go. The harder problem is figuring out where to go in the first place.

This leads to questions about survival how long does a flyer last, exposure how many people engage with a board, and learning how do you estimate value for locations you've never tried. These are the subjects of the companion paper, which develops a Bayesian hierarchical framework combining survival analysis, spatial point processes, and multi-armed bandits.

Preview Research

Route optimization is only the first layer. The deeper question which locations are worth visiting requires estimating the expected value of placing a flyer at each candidate site. This gets involved:

  • Survival analysis How long does a flyer last before removal. We use interval-censored observations via the Turnbull NPMLE and Weibull AFT models.
  • Spatial point processes Inhomogeneous Poisson processes with non-stationary thinning to model legal and institutional removal dynamics across Cambridge.
  • Multi-armed bandits Gaussian-process UCB and Thompson Sampling for sequential allocation of flyers to boards with spatial correlation.
  • Bayesian hierarchical model A three-level model fusing survival, exposure, and spatial effects into a posterior conversion utility heatmap.
The mathematical framework is documented in a companion paper. Every photograph collected on a bike ride is an interval-censored observation. Over time, the empirical posterior sharpens, the bandit explores beyond the seed set, and the city itself becomes the experiment.
